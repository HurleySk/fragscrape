Citrus(es) in Orbit
Jacques Cavallier-Belletrud, the in-house perfumer for Louis Vuitton, is the mastermind behind Météore. His style often blends purity with power, and that duality is clear here. Cavallier-Belletrud is known for crafting some of the most elegant and commercially successful scents in the modern era, including classics like Issey Miyake L’Eau d’Issey, Bvlgari Aqua, and more recently the entire Louis Vuitton lineup... from Ombre Nomade to Imagination. His precision with citrus and aquatic notes shines in Météore, striking a perfect balance between freshness and sophistication.

Among the wave of fragrances inspired by the fresh, slightly spicy, and musky blueprint made famous by scents like Abercrombie & Fitch Fierce and Parfums de Marly Percival, Louis Vuitton Météore stands out as a refined take on this beloved out-of-the-shower DNA. While the structural similarities to Fierce and Percival are present (i.e. sparkling citrus, herbal undertones, and a woody-ambery base) Météore distinguishes itself with its natural-smelling citrus top notes.

Right from the first spray, there’s a burst of Calabrian bergamot and Sicilian orange that feels juicy, bright, and organic. This realistic citrus character gives Météore a distinct elegance. The supporting notes of pink pepper, clary sage, and nutmeg bring aromatic warmth and a lightly spicy heart that complements the fresh opening without overwhelming it. As it settles, you get a smooth vetiver and amber wood base that extends its wear time without dragging it into heavy territory.

Performance is notably impressive for a freshie. 6 to 8 hours of longevity with moderate projection and a fresh, clean trail that makes it ideal for warm weather, and also professional settings. It wears effortlessly, leaning more mature than Fierce and more nuanced than Percival.

As a final note, with many Louis Vuitton fragrances, authenticity can be a significant issue.
A large number of counterfeits on the market often skew public perception of performance and quality. Unfortunately, LV fragrances can only be bought directly from their boutiques/website, so
the secondary market is risky.

Good soup
To preface, a fragrance for everyone this is not. It is something that you can boil down to being a fancier “blue” fragrance. It’s a branch off that same tree of shower gels and reed diffusers. It’s not a fragrance I can in good faith recommend for the price if it’s not what you’re looking for. That being said, I can say this is a fragrance that completely obliterates anything else on that tree.

That being out of the way, this fragrance opens gorgeous yet almost too clean. The first time you smell it you would think you’re smelling a bottle of one of those men’s body washes what some stupid name like “sports blast”, after about 10 minutes as that neroli comes in this evolves into the most gorgeous clean scent I’ve gotten my nose on. Every time you catch another whiff of it, you get a hint of something almost warm and powdery, the scent slowly gets enveloped in an embrace of cardamom.

As you make your way down to the heart of the fragrance the fragrance is almost lying in a bed of cardamom, while not the most prominent its there to stay, and that’s not a bad thing. Traveling even further, you start to get hints of that Sicilian orange and the pepper notes. While the orange is a welcome addition I can safely say it’s a perfect choice to keep it in the background. This it not a fragrance that needs a lot of citrus in it.

The base of Météore, though not all that dissimilar from the heart, is almost tucked into that bed of nutmeg and vetiver. And not that almost cooked pasta sort of vetiver but rather a light earthy sort of vetiver that works beautifully with the slightly bitter warm nutmeg balances out the cardamom.

Overall This fragrance has solicited itself in not just one of my favorite Louis Vuitton fragrances but also as my new signature scent.

Scent 8.9/10 - 9.5/10 in regards to freshies

Versatility 9.2/10 - can be used in almost any situation but would require an outfit to be built around it in some circumstances

Sillage 8.7/10 - For the first 6 or so hours you’ll have a good arm length sillage around you with 4 sprays

Durability 9.4/10 - easy 9+ hours on my skin, which is very impressive as most freshies I wear disappear in a matter of a couple hours.

Value 9.7/10 - The value for money of this fragrance is so high because this is the type of fragrance you buy not for a collection but for you, its worth as much as you want it to be and on top of that Louis Vuitton fragrances hold their value so incredibly well that I would say its nearly blind buy worth for that alone.

All I really have left to say about this fragrance is that is truly is what “Designer” fragrance should be. Météore is, like many other of its siblings and cousins stand at the pinnacle of what designer fragrance should and could be. Rather than copy and paste of the same flanker I’m truly able to see Louis Vuitton as a house rather than a Brand.

The worst of LV
I've gone back and forth on originality in perfumery. On one hand it's important to me to wear something innovative, and interesting; something that will captivate me. On the other hand it's also good to approach perfumery with one foot in the past, even to appeal to the enthusiast. If a perfumer creates a perfume that is very far removed from anything else that has come before, it just won't be enjoyable, since you won't have a reference point.

But I do know that I've always been against perfumery with two feet firmly in the past, and especially when it costs a lot of money. There is not one thing in Météore that has not been done before, and not just once or twice or three times before, but so many fragrances that sought to capitalize on the success of CK One Eau de Toilette and Fierce Cologne. It's just a combination of accords from those two fragrances. The neroli-based soapy laundry from CK One, and the ozonic freshness from Fierce.

And not only is it boring, it is old-fashioned. Météore is what boys' locker rooms smelled like in the mid-00s. And I should know, I was there. This is essentially recreating a dad scent. It would be like if a brand released a fragrance that smelled like a mix of Polo Eau de Toilette and Drakkar Noir Eau de Toilette in the year 2000. Why would anyone under the age of 35 buy it?

For a premium-priced product that is unavailable at discounters, it is incredibly synthetic and chock-full of low-quality ingredients. The neroli is not crisp and vibrant, the citruses in the opening burn off within seconds. I honestly don't get any of the actual notes except the citruses and neroli. The base is woody-musky aromachemicals that don't smell like vetiver. Cardamom and nutmeg are nonexistent. And someone might say, "well this DNA can't really be made non-synthetically," and that's entirely my point. Why make a fragrance with this DNA if you're not going to significantly update it and make it modern? Why should I be paying a premium price for what is essentially a 30-year-old drugstore scent?
